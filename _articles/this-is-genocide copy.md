# Framing, Agency, and Empathy: Linguistic Patterns in Palestine/Israel News Headlines

_A structural analysis for journalists and media professionals_

---

## Introduction

Readers rarely reach the article body. Most encounter a headline in a push notification, a social media preview, or a search result snippet, and that first line becomes the story their brain carries forward. Who acted. Who suffered. Who was credible. In no contemporary conflict does this matter more than in coverage of Palestine and Israel, where a set of recurring editorial habits has produced a quiet but measurable disparity in how harm is presented, and by extension, how readily it can be mourned.

This analysis identifies four patterns: the assignment of grammatical agency, the application of moral labels, the use of symmetry language, and the practice of humanization. It examines how their consistent, asymmetric application shapes audience empathy. A caveat up front. This is not a conspiracy argument. Newsrooms work under genuine constraints of time, space, wire service dependency, and house style. Nor does the asymmetry run uniformly in one direction across all outlets at all times. But the patterns are real, they are well-documented, and they can be addressed as what they are: editorial choices.

---

## Pattern 1: Who Acts, Who Dies

The grammar of harm is not neutral.

Whether a death is reported in active or passive voice, and which verb is chosen, encodes assumptions about agency and culpability that readers absorb before they've processed a single fact. This might sound like an overly technical point. It isn't.

Consider a headline that became notorious in media criticism circles. The _New York Times_, on July 10, 2014, reported an Israeli missile strike on a cafe in Gaza where patrons were watching the World Cup with this: **"Missile at Beachside Gaza Cafe Finds Patrons Poised for World Cup."** The missile is the grammatical subject, and its verb, "finds," makes it sound as though it merely dropped by. No agent fires it. No one is killed in the headline. The human catastrophe exists only as implication. After public outcry, the _Times_ revised it, not to something clearer, but to something even more opaque: **"Boys Drawn to Gaza Beach, and Into Center of Mideast Strife."** Now there is no missile, no blast, no Israeli military. Only boys mysteriously "drawn" toward "strife," as if they were participants in a geopolitical phenomenon rather than children killed on a beach by an airstrike.

Compare that with the _Associated Press_ headline from October 9, 2023, reporting the Hamas attack on the Nova music festival: **"Israeli survivors recount terror at music festival, where Hamas militants killed at least 260."** Here the grammar is fully transitive. Hamas militants are the subject. "Killed" is direct and agentive. The context, a music festival, survivors, terror, saturates the headline with moral weight before a single detail is read. It is, by any journalistic standard, the better headline. It names who did what to whom.

The telling contrast is that this standard is not consistently applied. A 2021 AP headline, **"Rockets kill 2 Israelis; 28 die in Gaza as Israel hits Hamas,"** performs the asymmetry within a single line. Israelis are killed by rockets, active, agent named. Palestinians simply "die," a verb that requires no subject and assigns no cause. A reader finishing that headline knows who killed the two Israelis. The 28 deaths in Gaza float free of any grammatical perpetrator, even as the clause "Israel hits Hamas" sits nearby, carefully separated by syntax.

The _PBS NewsHour_ (via AP) headline, **"Palestinian boy dies after being shot by Israeli soldiers during raid, health officials say,"** sits somewhere in between, and is worth pausing on. "Shot by Israeli soldiers" does name an agent, which is an improvement over the constructions above. But "dies after being shot" maintains a causal distance that "Israeli soldiers kill Palestinian boy during raid" would not. The perpetrator is present but recessed. And the sentence ends on an attribution, "health officials say," that frames the death as a claim still under evaluation. Name present, agency softened, death pending verification.

What accumulates across a news cycle is not any single editorial decision. It's a moral grammar, learned by repetition. One population's deaths require explanation and attribution; the other's require mourning. No individual journalist writes that rule. The pattern writes it for them.

Which raises a question worth carrying into the next section. If the issue were simply one of grammatical habit, we'd expect moral labels to be applied more evenly. They aren't.

## Pattern 2: Moral Labels and Who Gets Them

"Terrorist." "Massacre." "Slaughter." These are not descriptive words. They are verdicts. They tell readers how to feel before the facts have had a chance to do that work. The question is not whether such language is ever appropriate. Sometimes it clearly is. The question is whether it gets applied by anything resembling a consistent standard.

The _ABC News_ live war blog in October 2023 described the Hamas attack in the following terms: "Hamas terrorists began a full-fledged surprise attack… Hundreds of armed Hamas fighters stormed into Israel from Gaza, charging into cities and gunning down citizens." Three moral categories operating at once. "Terrorists," "surprise attack," "citizens," each doing evaluative work before the reader has encountered a single verified detail. The language is not wrong, necessarily. Hamas did attack. People were killed. But the construction is worth naming for what it is: moral saturation. It tells you the verdict first.

Palestinian deaths, by contrast, are frequently introduced through institutional attribution. "Health officials say." "Palestinians say." The effect is to move a death from the realm of established fact into the realm of claim to be evaluated, and it happens routinely enough that it looks less like verification practice and more like editorial reflex. Reuters's standard construction pairing death counts with "Palestinians say" is not false attribution. Conflict zone verification is genuinely difficult. But the same publications that introduce Palestinian casualties as disputed claims often do not apply equivalent hedging to Israeli casualty figures. And they rarely note who "health officials" actually are, namely the Gaza Health Ministry, whose figures Israel's own government has at times described as roughly accurate.

The al-Ahli Hospital episode in October 2023 is instructive, if complicated. The _New York Times_ initially published: **"Israeli Strike Kills Hundreds in Hospital, Palestinians Say."** When Israeli intelligence disputed the cause, the headline revised twice, migrating from "Israeli Strike" to "Strike on Gaza Hospital" to simply "Blast at Gaza Hospital," each revision further from a named agent. The Times later acknowledged in an editors' note that the initial coverage relied too heavily on unverified Hamas claims. That is a real and important editorial failure, and it deserves the criticism it received. But notice the direction. A high-profile error that overstated Palestinian claims prompted institutional correction, an editors' note, extensive public discussion. The quieter, more routine pattern of grammatically obscuring Palestinian casualties, the "dies amid," the "health officials say," the passive constructions accumulating across thousands of headlines, generates no equivalent response. There is no editors' note for a habit.

Perhaps the cleanest single example of the label gap is the _Washington Post_ headline on the killing of journalist Shireen Abu Akleh: **"With the killing of Shireen Abu Akleh, yet another Palestinian journalist dies on the job."** Abu Akleh was shot dead by Israeli military fire, a finding confirmed by the United Nations, the Palestinian Authority, and the U.S. State Department. "Dies on the job" is the language of occupational hazard. It strips the act of its agent as thoroughly as any passive construction. It is genuinely difficult to imagine an equivalent headline appearing for a journalist killed by Hamas. "Yet another Israeli journalist dies on the job" has never, to this author's knowledge, been written.

This asymmetry is not a matter of which deaths are covered. Both are covered. It's a matter of which deaths are named, and how.

## Pattern 3: "Clashes," Symmetry, and What Gets Erased

"Clashes" is among the most useful words in conflict journalism, which is precisely why it deserves scrutiny.

When violence is reported as "clashes," the headline implies two comparable forces in mutual collision. That implication distributes moral responsibility evenly, removes the question of who initiated what, and suggests a kind of spontaneous bilateral chaos with no grammatical subject and no one to hold accountable. "Clashes erupt" especially. Eruptions happen, like weather. Nobody causes them.

In coverage of Palestinian deaths, this construction appears disproportionately. The _AP_ headline, **"4 Palestinians killed by Israeli fire in fresh West Bank violence. Clashes erupt in flashpoint town,"** contains both a direct attribution ("killed by Israeli fire") and a symmetry frame ("clashes erupt") within the same two-sentence unit. The first sentence tells you what happened. The second muddies it. And notably, the article body identifies the dead as including armed gunmen who had engaged Israeli troops, a material distinction the headline makes no effort to convey. So the framing problem here cuts more than one way. "Clashes" isn't just erasing Palestinian civilian deaths, it's also erasing a factual distinction that would have complicated the headline's implied narrative. Imprecision, in other words, serves multiple evasions simultaneously.

Media critics have documented this pattern in detail. FAIR's 2018 analysis of Great March of Return coverage found that Israeli troops fired live ammunition at protesters from a distance, killing thirty Palestinians while sustaining no casualties, and multiple outlets described the incident as "clashes." When the funeral procession for Shireen Abu Akleh was attacked by Israeli forces with stun grenades and batons, an event captured on video from multiple angles, several outlets reached again for "clashes." A confrontation in which one party has military hardware, rules of engagement, and state authority, and the other does not, is not a clash in any meaningful sense. It's a "clash" in the sense that the word fits the space and avoids the editorial discomfort of naming what actually happened.

The contrast is visible in Reuters's headline, **"Car ramming, stabbing attack in West Bank kills one, injures three,"** where there is no symmetry language, no eruption, no "clashes." Just an attack, victims, and an outcome. Clean, direct, attributable. The same wire service. Different template.

That difference in templates is not random, and it doesn't disappear by pointing to individual editorial decisions. It's structural.

## Pattern 4: Who Gets Named, Who Gets Counted

There's a well-established finding in moral psychology. Slovic calls it the "collapse of compassion." Identified individuals generate stronger empathic response than anonymous statistical groups. A single named child with a photograph moves people in ways that a death toll does not. Journalism has always known this. The question is who gets the photograph.

Coverage of October 7 consistently individualized Israeli victims: festival-goers at a celebratory event, survivors describing fear in their own words, hostages with names and family roles visible in their Instagram profiles and their parents' pleas to camera. This is good journalism. It creates the conditions for readers to register human loss rather than abstract harm. The problem isn't that it was done. The problem is the comparison.

The _New York Times_ headline from May 2021, **"More Than 30 Dead in Gaza and Israel as Fighting Quickly Escalates,"** illustrates the issue efficiently. Thirty-five of those dead were Palestinian; five were Israeli. The symmetrical framing ("Gaza and Israel") and the passive construction ("dead") flatten a highly asymmetric reality into a shared toll, distributed evenly as if the conflict were producing roughly equivalent casualties on each side. It wasn't. But the headline made it look as though it might be.

A 2014 _Times_ story about a 10-year-old Palestinian boy shot at the Gaza border ran without specifying his age, a known fact that was simply not included. Whether that omission was deliberate or careless, its effect is the same. A detail that would have made the death immediately legible was absent.

The PBS/AP headline, "Palestinian boy dies after being shot by Israeli soldiers during raid, health officials say," does something subtler. It names the boy's existence through his death, but frames that death through the institution reporting it rather than through his life, his family, or his context. This is not a sin unique to Palestinian coverage. Breaking news often traffics in institutional attribution. But when one side's victims are consistently reached through survivors and family members and their own words, while the other side's victims are consistently reached through "health officials say" and aggregate counts, audiences absorb a lesson that no reporter consciously wrote. One suffering is real and immediate. The other is information to be assessed.

## Toward a More Rigorous Standard

The four patterns described here do not require malicious intent to produce meaningful harm. They emerge from convention, from wire dependency, from templates reproduced on deadline without interrogation. The consequence is a news environment in which Palestinian suffering is routinely rendered as more ambiguous, more contested, and less morally urgent than Israeli suffering, not because reporters decide this, but because the default outputs of their institutional habits produce it.

For working journalists, the corrective is not a new ideology. It's a consistent standard. The same grammatical directness applied to Israeli victims should be applied to Palestinian victims. The same verification skepticism applied to Palestinian claims should be applied to Israeli claims. The same institutional willingness to name an initiator, rather than retreat to "clashes" or "strife," should govern incidents regardless of who initiated them.

That standard is not currently met. Achieving it would require newsrooms to audit their own output for patterns rather than evaluating individual headlines in isolation. A single headline using "dies" instead of "was killed" is a judgment call. A thousand such headlines, trending in the same direction, is a policy, even if no one ever wrote it down.

---

## Conclusion

Let me be direct about something this kind of analysis usually leaves implicit.

The patterns described here are not symmetrical mistakes. They do not reflect a news culture that occasionally slips in both directions with equal frequency. They reflect a news culture in which certain editorial habits, passive voice, symmetry language, institutional attribution, aggregate counts, are applied more readily when the victims are Palestinian, and in which the moral vocabulary of outrage, "terror," "massacre," "attack," "citizens," is deployed more readily when the victims are Israeli. That is a structural tilt. It is not corrected by noting that individual reporters try to be fair, or that some outlets do better than others, or that covering this conflict is genuinely difficult. All of those things are true and none of them changes the pattern.

Headlines are not neutral containers for facts. They assign agency, credibility, and emotion, and in Palestine/Israel coverage they do so with a consistency that has real consequences for public understanding. When Palestinian suffering is framed with passive verbs, institutional attribution, symmetry language, and aggregate counts, while Israeli suffering is framed with active verbs, moral labels, direct identification, and individual faces, audiences learn a grammar. One death is a tragedy to be reckoned with. The other is a statistic to be evaluated.

This is not an argument that Israeli victims deserve less grief or less clarity. It is an argument that Palestinian victims deserve the same standard, the same direct verbs, the same presumptive credibility, the same individualizing care, that serious journalism is already capable of providing, and has demonstrated it can provide, when it decides to.

Consistency is both an ethical and a craft value. The framing problem described here is a fairness problem, a quality problem, and an accuracy problem. It is also, and this matters, a solvable one. The tools already exist. The question is whether the profession is willing to apply them without regard to whose death is being described.
